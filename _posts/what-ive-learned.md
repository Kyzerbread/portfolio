If you are a new or casual developer looking for some insights on how to improve your skills, here are some of my tips that I've picked up through my 4+ years experience in the industry.

## 1. Understand the Industry

When starting your journey, I'ts important to familiarize yourself with all the major players and technologies affecting and supporting the industry. For example, you should know about Amazon Web Services, some Google APIs/Firebase, and Microsoft Azure. These are the major hosting and web service providers. Amazon alone powers a whopping 1/3 of every website on the internet. There is a virtual gurantee you will be working with at least one of these in your career no matter where you end up working. Modern companies don't have you sit at a desk and program in your IDE all day. You WILL be using dozens of different programs and services to track your work, deploy your code, coordinate with your team, troubleshoot, and many perform other tasks.

Other notable services you should check out are Twilio, Jira, Github, Gitlab, and Docker.

## 2. Specalize

You've probably heard of the pharse "Jack of All Trades" before. Some developers think the most effective way to be employable and a "good programmer" is to know many languages, dozens frameworks, all the tech stacks, all the design patters, etc. This is a common misconception and a HUGE waste of time. Let's put this into perepective. There are currently about 700 programming languages, 24 major JS frameworks, over 200 AWS web services, and ~343 different databases to choose from. Secondly, they are constanly evolving and there are new ones being released every month. Essentially being a Jack of All Trades is impossible as there are too many in this industry to handle for any normal human.

It is up to you to determine which area to focus in, to learn just the associated technologies, and practice your skills at. Companies will value you more if you have an area where you shine in over someone whos just a generic developer. They desire people who can bring something to the table that few others can. If youre a web developer maybe focus React and Redux. If you want to be backend maybe focus on a specific database such as MongoDB. Learn them in and out and build some applications with them to a degree that really demonstrates you don't just use them, but you understand them at a deep level. Think about how it would look on a resume. I've seen people cram as many languages, technologies, and skills into theirs as possible. It appears significantly better to have a handful of skills you are very confident in, than dozens of them you are mediocre ok at.

## 3. Know Your Limits

You've likely already experienced this. You are programming at desk solving problems and writing algorithms. After about 8ish hours youll likely feel mentally tired, foggy, and slow. DO NOT KEEP PROGRAMMING, seriously. The brain is a lot like a muscle and will fatigue with use just like any other part of the body. Your inner dialog may tell you that you can keep going for 10, 12, or even 14 hours a day. This is horseshit. My most productive days have been after a good night sleep, excersize, and effective periods of relaxation. Day after day of non stop programming leads to sloppy and error prone code. Know your limits, take breaks, and do something other than coding in your spare time. Check out [Mosh Hamedani's take](https://programmingwithmosh.com/general/why-coders-should-work-5-hours-a-day/) on why programmers are only effective for about 5 hours a day max. Time spent programming is effective for about 5 hours, after that you're better off taking a significant break, than if you were to just keep going for hours on end.
